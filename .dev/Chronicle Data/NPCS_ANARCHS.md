## Red Flag

- Status (Anarchs): 4

- the diplomatic arm of the Anarchs, serving as emissaries to the Camarilla and Hecata, led by the Nosferatu Rosie who has earned respect from all sides
- membership includes Damien Abanda, childe of the current prince, Lord Lucien St. Claire

### Player Introduction to Red Flag

Red Flag is the difficult conversation you cannot avoid. This coterie serves as the Anarch Movement's formal emissary to the Camarilla and the Hecata; their mission is to further Anarch interests in Toronto while avoiding outright war. Red Flag is particularly adept at negotiating with the Camarilla, as they possess all of the courtesy and courtly graces your typical Camarilla vampire never expects of Anarchs. Indeed, their membership includes the childe of your prince, Lord Lucien—seated across the table as proof that sectarian lines are no longer clean. Do not expect Red Flag to posture as revolutionaries when they meet with you: They will instead come armed with an easy familiarity with the etiquette of the Court, and a keen understanding of leverage. How long before their calm insistence begins to define the limits of your authority?

**They represent your greatest rivals in Toronto: They exist to ensure you don't forget that this city is not yours to control.**

## The Redeemers

- Status (Anarchs): 3

An Anarch coterie of four **Ministry** vampires. Note that the currently-written bios were written for the previous chronicle, "Toronto By Night", when Ren and his three childer all operated out of "Redemption House".

- In *Toronto By Night*, **Ren** (boss of the Redeemers) opened **Redemption House** in the Danforth ~8 years ago. Their pitch to addicts: treat one dependency by indulging in *other* vices—dilute fixation through curated hedonism, sold as "rebalancing."
- In *Toronto Rising*, the current Chronicle, Ren's childer have splintered into their own facilities across Toronto:
  - **Tyler (eldest childe): Remembrance House** — Nostalgia as panacea: drug-fueled "memory pilgrimages" and escalating trips into the client's past, where they "recover" lost memories and gain truth from the recollection (it's an open question the degree to which these memories are manufactured by the therapy)
  - **Alexandra (middle childe): Reconciliation House** — Mediation-by-euphoria: drugs, celebration, and ecstatic group "therapy" that becomes orgiastic.
  - **Kai (youngest childe): Revelation House** — Psychedelics + extreme therapies (hypothermic saunas, stimulant endurance routines, all-night dance-a-thons) to break down barriers and surface buried truths. The most unabashedly hedonistic of the four Houses of Redemption, on many nights it rivals the club scene for the revelry on site
- All four Houses are popular and (publicly) "successful," in part because the full truth of the therapies is rarely admitted to—clients describe results, not methods.

### Player Introduction to the Redeemers

The Redeemers offer a rare thing in Toronto. A framework for recovery that does not shame hunger, and does not pretend desire disappears when you scold it. Ren's original Redemption House on the Danforth proved the model: dependency weakens when the self is given breadth again, when the psyche stops clinging to one narrow relief and learns to move through many forms of pleasure with intention. Now the work has matured. The Houses have diversified, each refining a distinct therapeutic lens for a distinct kind of suffering. Their clients do not speak in lurid details. They speak in outcomes. They sleep. They eat. They forgive. They return to their families. They stop shaking. How long before you're forced to admit that their methods work on people you could never hope to reach?

**They represent your greatest rivals in Toronto: Four Snakes coiled around your city, spreading influence through unassailable compassion and unimpeachable good will.**

## The Line

- Status (Anarchs): 3

### Player Introduction to the Line

The Anarchs of the Line does not hold salons. They do not preach revolution, and they don't chase recognition. What the Line does is succinctly explained by their name: they hold the line. Charged by the Good Doctors—and by Baroness Eulenberg before them—with securing Toronto's borders, they decide who passes through Toronto's arteries and who is quietly turned back into the night. There is perhaps no coterie in Toronto with greater expertise on the city's bones, its pulsing infrastructure and the mechanisms of controlling it. With or without the consent of the Camarilla, the Line claims Union Station's labyrinthine corridors, the subterranean veins of the subway and P.A.T.H, the shifting threshold between waterfront and city, and even the neutral territory of Yonge Street as their domain.
Under Sage Sam's silent prophecies and Mason Schmidt's field command, the Line has become very, very good at what they do. Couriers move by their say-so, and visitors arrive and depart on their watch. In a city where the Second Inquisition watches the skies and princes test the ground beneath their feet, someone must decide what crosses the threshold, and the Line has decided that "someone" is them. How long before you must confront the possibility that there may not be anyone else who can do their job?

**They represent your greatest rivals in Toronto: Self-appointed custodians of its borders, and the one Anarch coterie who may be truly indispensable.**

Young in appearance and young at heart, he's charmingly cagey when it comes to questions about his age. Is he really, as some whisper, a mighty elder laying low? Or is he a mere neonate enjoying such grandiose suspicions? And his Malkavian lineage invites another question: Does *he* even know? Whatever the case, Old Quentin has served the baroness ably and loyally, and as long as he continues to do so, she's hardly one to point fingers at the peculiarities of others.
